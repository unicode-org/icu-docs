Q. How many senses of "lowercase" does the Unicode Standard have?

A. Three distinct senses:

  • The Lowercase property. This forms the broadest set of characters, and indicates that a character is lowercase in form.
  • The Lowercase_Letter value of the General_Category property. These are also based on form, but are restricted. The General_Category is a partition of Unicode. If a Lowercase character is not primarily used as a letter, then it won't be in this set.
  • Functionally Lowercase. Characters may be lowercase in form, but if they have no case mappings and are never the result of a case mapping, then they are functionally caseless -- they act like uncased letters. (For more information, see Section 3.13 in the Unicode Standard.) The functionally lowercase characters are those that are functionally cased and the result of the toLowercase operation.

Q. Sigh. So what does that give me?

A. Let's take the simplest case first. There is no Titlecase property, and the set of Titlecase_Letter characters is exactly the same as the above set of 31 Functionally Titlecase characters.

Q. How about Uppercase?

A. Parallel to lowercase, there is an Uppercase property, and also the Uppercase_Letter value in the General_Category. However, these do not distinguish the characters that are Uppercase but not Titlecase. So for comparison, we'll lump in the Functionally Uppercase and Titlecase into the Functionally Uppercase category. Uppercase then looks very similar to lowercase: the main difference is that there are no characters with just the Uppercase property. Notice that Uppercase is a superset of the other two, but that the other two are disjoint.
